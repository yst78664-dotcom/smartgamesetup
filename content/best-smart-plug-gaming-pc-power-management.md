## Smart Plug Safety Tips for Gaming PCs

### Check Your Wattage

Most smart plugs are rated for 15A / 1800W. A high-end gaming PC (RTX 4090 + i9-14900K) can draw 600-800W from the wall under load. Add a monitor (30-80W) and peripherals (20-50W), and you're well within the 1800W limit. However, **never plug a space heater or high-draw appliance into the same smart plug** as your gaming PC.

### Use Surge Protection

Smart plugs are **not** surge protectors. Always plug your smart plug into a surge-protected outlet or UPS, or use a surge-protecting smart power strip. Your $2,000 GPU is not worth saving $20 on surge protection.

### Don't Hard-Kill While Gaming

Use smart plug scheduling to kill power when you know your PC is off or in sleep mode. Cutting power while Windows is running can cause data corruption. Set up automations that trigger *after* you've shut down — like a 30-minute timer after your PC enters low power draw.

### Enable Away Mode

Going on vacation? Use your smart plug's scheduling to cycle your setup on and off at random intervals. This simulates occupancy and has the bonus of preventing any power draw when truly not needed.

## FAQ

### Can a smart plug damage my gaming PC?

No. A smart plug is simply an on/off switch for the power outlet. It doesn't modify voltage, amperage, or anything else about the power delivery. It's electrically identical to unplugging your PC from the wall and plugging it back in. The only risk is cutting power while the PC is running, which can cause data corruption — but that's a usage issue, not a hardware risk.

### Is it safe to cut power to a PC that's in sleep mode?

Generally yes. When a PC is in sleep mode (S3), it stores the session in RAM and uses minimal power. Cutting power will lose the sleep session, but your PC will simply do a fresh boot next time — no damage or data corruption. However, if you use **hibernate** (S4), the session is saved to disk, and cutting power is perfectly safe since the drive isn't being written to.

### How much money can a smart plug actually save?

It depends on your setup's phantom draw and your electricity rate. A typical gaming setup draws 30-50W in standby (PC sleep + monitor standby + peripherals). At the US average of $0.16/kWh, that's about $42-70/year. A smart plug that kills all standby draw saves you that amount annually — paying for itself in 2-3 months.

### Do I need one smart plug per device or one for my whole setup?

For most gamers, one smart plug on a power strip is sufficient — kill power to the entire strip and everything connected to it turns off. However, if you want energy monitoring per device (to know how much your PC vs. monitor vs. speakers each draw), you'd need individual plugs.

### Will a smart plug work with a UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)?

Yes, but placement matters. Put the smart plug **before** the UPS (wall → smart plug → UPS → PC). This way, the smart plug controls power to the UPS, and the UPS still protects your PC from surges and brief outages. Never put a smart plug between the UPS and the PC.

### What's the difference between Matter and non-Matter smart plugs?

Matter is a universal smart home standard that works across all ecosystems (Alexa, Google, Apple, SmartThings). Non-Matter plugs typically only work with one or two ecosystems. If you might switch platforms in the future, or if you have a mixed ecosystem household, Matter plugs are worth the small premium.

### Can I use a smart plug to turn ON my PC?

Not directly — a smart plug can restore power to the outlet, but your PC won't boot automatically unless you enable "Power On After AC Loss" in your BIOS settings. With this enabled, restoring power via smart plug will trigger a boot. Check your motherboard's BIOS for this setting (sometimes called "Restore on AC Power Loss" or "AC Back").
